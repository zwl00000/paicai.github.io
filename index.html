Overview

Modern machine learning systems are predominantly trained and evaluated under closed-world assumptions: i.i.d. data distributions, fixed label spaces, and abundant clean annotations. Real-world deployments, however, operate in open, dynamic, and resource-constrained environments where models must handle label noise, distribution shifts, unseen concepts, and evolving tasks.

OWRML'26 focuses on principled algorithms for robust learning in open-world scenarios, bridging theoretical guarantees with practical deployment needs. The workshop aligns with PRICAI 2026's emphasis on trustworthy, generalizable, and deployment-ready AI, and aims to foster cross-disciplinary dialogue on how to build models that remain reliable when the world refuses to stay closed.

Objectives & Scope

Unify Research Streams

Bring together fragmented research on robust learning under supervision scarcity, distribution shift, and environmental dynamics.

Showcase New Algorithms

Highlight methods that jointly optimize robustness, adaptability, and data efficiency in open-world environments.

Bridge Theory & Practice

Connect theoretical guarantees with real-world case studies, industry challenges, and open research roadmaps.

Topics of Interest

Learning from Imperfect Supervision

  • Weakly supervised learning
  • Noisy-label, partial-label, and complementary-label learning
  • Crowdsourcing, active learning, and semi-supervised learning

Robustness to Distribution Shift & Openness

  • Out-of-distribution learning and open-set recognition
  • Domain generalization, domain adaptation, and test-time adaptation
  • Novel class discovery and robust representation learning

Data-Efficient & Lifelong Adaptation

  • Continual and lifelong learning
  • Few-shot and zero-shot learning
  • Concept drift, resource constraints, and deployment-ready adaptation

The workshop especially welcomes work on algorithmic robustness, theoretical convergence or identifiability guarantees, scalable implementations, fairness, calibration, and uncertainty quantification.

Target Audience

The workshop targets researchers in machine learning and deep learning, AI engineers deploying models in dynamic or noisy domains such as autonomous systems, healthcare, finance, NLP, and computer vision, as well as PhD students and postdoctoral scholars interested in robust and adaptive AI.
